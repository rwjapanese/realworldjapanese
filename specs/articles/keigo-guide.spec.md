---

## 1. Target & Intent

**Primary search intent:** Learn what keigo is AND how to actually use it in the Japanese workplace (not just grammar definitions). The searcher has likely encountered keigo at work/school and wants a practical reference, not an academic treatise.

**Audience persona:** An intermediate Japanese learner (JLPT N3–N2 level) who has recently started working in Japan or with Japanese colleagues and feels uncertain choosing the right register for different situations.

**Success criteria:**
- Reader walks away knowing the 3 keigo categories AND when to use which
- Reader can pick a register for at least 5 common workplace situations without guessing
- Reader knows the 2–3 most common mistakes non-native speakers make

## 4. Content Gaps

1. **Situation-first framing.** All top-10 lead with grammar taxonomy; none organize content around "what do I say when X happens at work?"
2. **Register selection framework.** Competitors describe what keigo IS but not HOW to pick the right level in-the-moment. A decision rule ("when in doubt, pick B") is absent from the entire SERP.
3. **Non-native pitfalls beyond grammar.** Top-10 mention grammar mistakes, but few cover sociolinguistic errors like *ryōkai-shimashita* to a client, over-use of *~sasete-itadaku*, or *gokurousama* to a superior.
4. **Digital-era register (Slack / email / Teams).** 0/10 competitors address where expats actually use keigo most today.
5. **Uchi-soto as a decision variable.** Only Tofugu goes deep; most competitors treat it as a cultural footnote rather than the axis that shapes A vs B vs C.
6. **JLPT bridge.** N3–N2 learners are a large segment. No competitor explicitly ties verb-conjugation tables to the JLPT grading context even though it would rank for those queries.
7. **FAQ schema.** Despite strong PAA presence, most competitors don't mark up an FAQ section — a clear technical-SEO gap.
8. **Interactive practice.** No competitor ships a quiz or self-diagnostic. (Future lead magnet candidate.)

## 5. Our Differentiation

- **A/B/C politeness framework.** Three-level register selector (Casual / Neutral-Polite / Formal) paired to 10 workplace scenarios. No competitor uses this or any equivalent heuristic.
- **"When in doubt, pick B"** explicit decision rule elevated to a takeaway. Fills the register-selection gap.
- **10 concrete workplace scenarios** (greeting, thanks, apology, request, decline, acknowledgement, question, excuse, closing, email opening) shown at all three levels side-by-side. The reference table competitors don't ship.
- **4 non-native pitfalls** (up from 3 in v1 after SERP refresh): *ryōkai* to clients, *gokurousama* to superiors, *sasete-itadaku* abuse, over-defaulting to C with peers. Each is a real-world friction point.
- **Uchi-soto as an operating axis**, not a footnote. Explicit call-out in a dedicated section.
- **Verb-conjugation quick-reference** aligned to the A/B/C framework, which gives us JLPT-study traffic without becoming a grammar drill page.
- **Romanization on every Japanese example** (Hepburn macrons + italic). Unique to us because our audience is actively reading Japanese.
- **Digital-register bias.** Slack, email opener, "reply to boss at 8pm" are the framing scenarios — competitors use classroom/in-person scenarios.
- **FAQ section marked up with FAQPage schema** (EN version). Captures PAA real estate that competitors leak.

## 10. Localization Notes

- **Terminology:** "keigo" stays romanized in EN; in JA it's 敬語 / 尊敬語 / 謙譲語 / 丁寧語 — use kanji. In VI/KO/ZH, consider whether to keep 敬語 or use a native equivalent (e.g. 敬語 "kính ngữ" in VI).
- **Cultural assumptions:** EN version assumes reader is a foreigner working AT a Japanese company. JA version assumes reader IS a foreigner who already speaks functional Japanese (tone shift: less English hedging, more assumed context).
- **Competitor landscape:** In VI/KO SERPs, expect different competitor mix — more likely academic/grammar-first sources, fewer practical workplace guides. Worth re-running localize skill before VI/KO launch.
- **Language-specific risks:** The A/B/C framework name is English-derived. In non-English articles, we should keep the A/B/C labels (numeric/latin markers travel well) but re-title the framework header locally (e.g. "3レベル丁寧度フレームワーク" for JA).
